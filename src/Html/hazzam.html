    Analysis of My Poems

     
     

    Analysis of Office Guard by Prof. Bilu.

    Here goes:

    The general atmosphere of this poem is dark and foreboding. While we still see a faint shadow of the poet's earlier style - the spaces before commas and periods - the evident use os a spell checker ruins much of the primal joy his earlier work had given us. A lone beacon in this aphotic umbra is the word, or more precisely, the concatenation of letters, "finalised".
    Obviously, as my esteemed colleague Mr. Shahar once noted, Hazzam can't spell shit, thus I am quite certain that the above observation, of a spell checker being used, must be true. But how can we explain this "finalised"?
    I contend that this is yet another example of the poet's genius. Among all these opaque, spell-checked words, we see  "finalised" shining through, as if saying "I am still the old poet you know and love, I still can't spell shit."
    Note also the unnecessary space, both before and after "finalised", as if to emphasize the poet's unique style.

    "Office guard" deals with the artist's feeling that his work (which might be a metaphor for life in general) is turning into a battle ground, hinting the final battle of Armageddon. The rest of this analysis will be dedicated to  unveiling the many military metaphors by which the poet dispenses his feelings of looming doom.
    First and foremost is the poem's title, "Office guard". Simply ingenious! The whole paradigm of the poem is folded into one short phrase - the consolidation of civil work, symbolized by "office", with military one - "guard", and the feeling of danger ahead:

    What does an office need a guard?
    Normally, it doesn't.
    Thus, Hazzam forces us to conclude that something terrible is about to happen, against which this protection is necessary.

    gideon@memco.co.il wrote:

      As some of you probably noticed , we have started to use a security company
      to secure our office .

    The first couplet starts softly, building our fear slowly. Pianissimo: "As some of you probably noticed". That is, the poet assures us that what he is about to say, is not news to us. It is something we already know, or at the very least,"noticed".
    The poet is saying: Did the title of this poem make you anxious? Relax. I am only about to explain something which you've already noticed on your own. He lures us into trusting him, only to make our revelation at the end much more powerful.

    The sentence continues with the elaborate grammar for which the poet is well known, and continues with a small joke, intending to calm us further. The joke, for those of you not familiar with Kasha Humor, is the double meaning of "security company".
    Memco is a security company, and "has started using" a security company - Memco might very well be hiring itself, entrapping itself in an endless loop.
    Is this the danger Hazzam is warning us against?
    I don't think so. This is merely a joke.

    However, the second line puts us on edge again. "Secure the office". What for? Against whom? Our questions are left dangling in the void between the two paragraphs.

     

     
      The plan is to have a guard as of 20:00 to 08:30 (evening and night shift)
      every day during the week , and 24 h a day during the weekends.
      Their base will be the main reception desk in the 3rd floor .
      Soon , all office security procedures will be  finalised ,  and the guard
      will be following them during his shift .
     

    The main part of the poem is dedicated into transforming the office, in our mind, from a civil work place to a military camp.

    The word shift is used twice, at the end of the first and last line. Again, this is an ingenious choice, since "shift" is both used to describe rotation of guards, and the time frame in which people work in an office (or at least a factory).

    So "shift" from the first line to the last is tying up the civil with the military, and shifts our state
    of mind between these two extremes as well.

    Another important point is the use of army language. This is evident by the use of digits instead of words when ever possible, the making of a "plan", the use of "procedures", the bad grammar ("have a guard as of"), the use of redundancy to give the illusion of power ("main reception desk" as if there's another, subsidiary, reception desk), and the explicit metamorphosis of a desk into a "base" and of corridors and rooms into patrol routes and guard posts.

    But what really happens here?
    Did we discover why the office needs guarding? What is the danger ahead?
    Not at all!
    More over, we got only a vague idea as to what the plan is, as the exact "office security procedures" are not described, maybe do not even exist.
    All that is implied, by the use of the word "all", is that there are many such procedures.

    I think the poet's intent is twofold:
    A vague plan with procedures which in part do not exist, and in part are kept secret, is characteristic of military plans - thus enhancing the "office transformed into army base" sentiment.
    But there is much more than this - the poet intentionally leaves us guessing, wondering, lost and afraid.

      Meanwhile , be aware of their present in the office .
     

    Last but not least, we are reminded that a spell checker is no help when the meaning of the words are not known.
    Since present is a word, the spell checker does not inform the poet that his readers would have expected the word "presence" instead.
    But is this really a mistake?
    Don't underestimate the Kasha.
    The present is in the office.
    The present of the nameless, faceless, guards.
    And also our own present.

    But what about the future?
    We are left with a terrible feeling about this unknown future, and stating that the present is in the office, leads usto suspect that the future is somewhere else.

    Indeed, the future of this humble literary critic is probably not in the office. At least, not this one.

    Take care, and congratulations to all of us who are getting married,
        Kasha Blue.
